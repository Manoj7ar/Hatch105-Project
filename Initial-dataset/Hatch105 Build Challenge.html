The opportunity

Thanks for taking this on. We're trying to work out the last couple of spots in Hatch this year, which is running with Happy Stack Labs (happystack.com/lab), and this challenge is how we're figuring that out. Nothing's set in stone on teams or roles yet. We just want to see how you think and build on a real problem.

Happy Stack Labs is the engine behind it. It researches the e-commerce and SaaS space, comes up with hundreds of startup ideas, and builds the best ones into real companies. This task is a small version of that job: take a pile of raw ideas, make a sharp call on what's worth building, and build something that gets you there.

The challenge

1

Take the 50 theses

50 real theses from the Happy Stack Labs research — narrative only (title, one-liner, example customer, the wedge). No scores, no rankings. Files: candidate_theses.csv / .json (this folder).

2

Build a system that ranks them

Which idea should a Hatch team this year actually build — something a 3-person team can stand up, get to revenue within 10 weeks, and grow into a real company? You pick the criteria and write them down, so anyone can see why an idea ranked where it did and where you made a judgment call. Don't deeply research all 50: use light research only where it changes the decision, source any real market number or competitor, and label guesses as guesses.

3

Make it work on new ideas, live

This is the spine of the whole thing. On the call we give you 5 new theses in the same format, and your system ingests them and returns an updated ranking of everything, with a short line on where each new one landed and why. If it only works because you hand-curated the first 50, it'll fall over here. That's intentional.

What this really tests

This isn't a hand-made ranking exercise. It's a test of whether you can build a small, inspectable system that makes good ranking decisions and updates when new ideas arrive.

How Hatch reads an idea

Choosing your own ranking criteria is part of the test, so this isn't a rubric to fill in. But these are the lenses we use, to point you at the right target. A great ranking has a clear view on questions like these, and the best candidates bring sharper ones we didn't list:

  • Buildability in a 10-week sprint: what could a 3-person team realistically stand up and ship — what's the v1 in 3 days · 3 weeks · 10 weeks?
  • Speed to revenue: realistic to make money inside 10 weeks? And which ideas could grow to €10K, then €1M long-term?

Reason about relative speed and feasibility. We're not after invented €-by-month forecasts, we're after judgment.

Definition of done

  • A working ranking system — any stack, any tools (AI encouraged).
  • A ranked list of all 50, with a short rationale for your top 3.
  • A brief explanation of your ranking criteria — the logic the system uses.
  • For your #1: 2–3 sentences on the smallest version you'd try to sell or ship first.
  • Live re-rank: on the call we give you 5 new theses in the same CSV/JSON format, and your system re-ranks the full set using the same logic (with a short rationale for where the new ones land), not a fresh hand-made judgment.
  • A 30-minute walkthrough call: show us how it works, then run our 5 live.
Ground rules

Budget 3–5 focused hours, this stops you overbuilding. A scrappy system that works beats a polished app: we care more about the ranking logic, your reasoning, and the live re-rank than the interface. The system should be repeatable — same criteria, broadly the same result, with any judgment calls visible. Use any tools and AI you like; if you use AI, keep the criteria and scoring logic inspectable.

What we're really looking for

Judgment
A real feel for what a 3-person team can build and sell, not just what scores big on paper.
Rigor
Evidence over vibes. You know which numbers are solid and which are guesses.
Systems
You built a machine, not a list. New input in, sensible ranking out.
Instinct
You can spot the idea that looks huge but is a trap for a small team, and say why.
One honest hint

Some of these look like obvious winners and aren't — the market's real, but building or selling it is a swamp for a small team. The best submissions don't just rank the easy wins; they catch the traps and explain them.

Logistics

Budget 3–5 focused hours, inside a 24-hour window. We'll do a quick background call first, then you'll have roughly 24 hours. Send us what you've got about an hour before the walkthrough so we can look it over first. Then a 30-minute call: you take us through it and we run 5 new theses live. Questions welcome, building the right thing beats guessing.
